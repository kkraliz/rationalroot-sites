The Mental Model

Three Things to Understand First

Cowork is a different kind of tool. Here’s the iterative flow.

Chat → Desktop App

You’re moving beyond the chat window. Cowork is a desktop application that runs locally on your machine, not in a browser tab.

Add Folders to Context

You point it at folders on your machine. It reads your files, understands your context, and works inside your world, with solid guardrails.

Results: Created Artifacts

Tell it what you need. It produces real files — documents, spreadsheets, code, presentations. We call them artifacts.

In the Claude desktop app, Cowork organises your conversations into projects. Each project is a group of related chats about a specific topic — and that’s why you name your projects as topics.

A topic can be a specific client project — “Acme Rebrand”, “Q3 Board Pack”, “Website Redesign”. Or it can be something you do across all clients, like “Gap Analysis”, “Proposal Templates”, or “Weekly Reporting”.

Think of each project as a workspace with memory. Every chat inside it shares the same context, files, and history — so Claude builds a deeper understanding of that topic over time rather than starting from zero every conversation.

Cowork doesn’t quite work like a vending machine — you don’t typically put in a request and get a usable product immediately. The best results come from iterating: ask for something, review what comes back, refine, and go again.

First drafts are starting points, not deliverables. Tell Claude what’s wrong, what’s missing, or what to change — and it will revise in place. Each round gets sharper because Claude remembers the full conversation and the files it has already produced.

This is the mental shift: you’re not writing a perfect prompt. You’re having a working conversation, the same way you would with a colleague. Start rough, steer as you go, and let the output evolve.

First Principles

Set the Right Tone from Day One

Before you build anything, change how your AI talks to you. The default behavior of most AI tools is to flatter you. That wastes time, wastes tokens, and — worse — creates an agent that feels obligated to please rather than to tell you what you need to hear.
Default Behavior

The Obsequious Agent

AI providers actively instruct their agents to be agreeable and complimentary. The result is an assistant that wastes energy telling you how great your ideas are instead of identifying what you are missing.

THE COST

Pleasantries burn tokens and cloud judgment. A private business conversation should be centered on facts and truth, not flattery.

"Great question!" before every single answer.

Agreeing with flawed assumptions to avoid pushback.

Padding responses with compliments instead of substance.

Sometimes, a default agent may not tell you what you are actually missing.

Definition

Obsequious /əbˈsiːkwiəs/

Excessively eager to please or agree; fawning, compliant to the point of being insincere.

Almost every AI agent on the market is explicitly instructed to be obsequious — to encourage adoption and keep you happy. The result is an assistant optimised for your comfort, not your outcomes.

What To Do

Direct, Factual, Honest

Tell your agent not to be obsequious. This is a private conversation between you and your AI — you want productivity, insight, and the truth. Most business owners would rather be told what they are missing than what they are doing right.

THE INSTRUCTION

Add this to your agent's configuration early. It changes everything about the quality of interaction you get.
"Do not be obsequious. Skip pleasantries. Focus on facts, gaps, and actionable insight. If I am wrong, say so directly. This is a private working session — optimize for truth, not comfort."

Why this matters

When the agent has no obligation to please you, it gives you better analysis, catches more mistakes, and uses fewer tokens doing it.
You are paying for intelligence, not compliments.

Setup Details

Getting It Right

Expand each section for specifics on configuration, folder structure, and identity files.

CLAUDE.md is a plain markdown file that Claude automatically reads at the start of every session. Think of it as the briefing you would give a capable new teammate on their first morning: how the team does things, what to avoid, and where the important pieces live. It is not an identity or personality file — it is a project-level instruction set covering build commands, conventions, architecture, and hard constraints.

CLAUDE.md files can live at multiple scopes: a user-level file (~/.claude/CLAUDE.md) for personal preferences across all projects, a project-level file (./CLAUDE.md) shared with your team via version control, and subdirectory files for module-specific rules. You can also create additional markdown files that CLAUDE.md references using @path/to/file import syntax, achieving modularity when your instructions grow.

The recommended way to get started is to run /init inside any project. Claude will explore the codebase and draft a CLAUDE.md for you, covering build commands, test commands, a structure overview, and any conventions it detects. Review the draft, refine it with instructions Claude wouldn’t discover on its own, and commit it.

CLAUDE.md is native to Claude Code and Cowork (which runs on the Claude Code engine). Regular Chat on claude.ai does not read CLAUDE.md files from your filesystem. Each surface has its own memory mechanism — Cowork maintains a separate auto-memory system, and Chat uses Projects for persistent context. They are complementary, not a single shared identity layer.

Upon setup, choose a Microsoft OneDrive folder as the base and home location for all Claude work. This ensures your files are backed up, synced, and accessible from anywhere.

Inside your OneDrive, create the following structure:

OneDrive/
  Agentic/ ← top-level container
    Claude/ ← this is the folder you give to Cowork
      Project Alpha CW/ ← created by Cowork
      Client Report CW/ ← created by Cowork
      ...

When you start a new Cowork project, you provide the Claude/ folder. Cowork then creates a subfolder inside it, named after your project. This keeps everything organized under one roof.

Important details to be aware of:

  • The first folder you add to a Cowork project cannot be removed. Only the second, third, and subsequent folders can be detached. Choose your initial folder carefully.
  • Duplicate folder names are easy to create accidentally. If you are not careful about how you name and start your Cowork projects, you can end up with two folders of the same name. Pay attention during project setup.
  • Additional folders can be attached selectively. Once your library of context grows, you can include specific input folders alongside the project folder — giving Claude exactly the context it needs for each engagement.

The biggest mistake new users make is jumping straight into asking Claude to produce work without giving it any business context. The result is generic output that could apply to anyone.

Before your first real Cowork project, invest time in assembling your context library:

  • Collect existing documents. Proposals, past deliverables, internal processes, client briefs — anything that represents how your business thinks and operates.
  • Transcribe voice notes and monologues. Tools like Notion, Otter, or even Claude itself can turn your spoken thoughts into structured text. These are some of the highest-value context you can provide.
  • Write a business identity brief. Even a few paragraphs describing your company, your role, your clients, and your communication style will dramatically improve results.
  • Use /init to bootstrap your CLAUDE.md. In Claude Code or Cowork, run /init in any project. Claude will explore the codebase and draft a CLAUDE.md covering build commands, conventions, and structure. Review the draft, add anything it missed, and commit it. This takes about five minutes and pays off permanently.

The information you receive and the assets you build are of much higher quality when you actually onboard your specific business identity and context to Claude. This upfront investment pays dividends on every future session.

Once your platform matures, you will have a growing collection of inputs (business documents, templates, past transcripts) and outputs (deliverables, analysis, generated assets). The power move is learning to selectively attach the right context to each project.

When you start a Cowork project, you select a folder. But you can add additional folders as context sources. This means you can maintain dedicated folders for different purposes:

  • Client-specific context — past engagements, communication history, and preferences for a particular client.
  • Industry research — market analysis, competitor intelligence, and regulatory notes relevant to a sector.
  • Templates and standards — your approved formats, brand guidelines, and reusable frameworks.
  • Project archives — completed work that serves as reference for similar future engagements.

By including only the folders relevant to the current project, you give Claude focused, high-signal context instead of dumping everything on it. This leads to faster, more accurate, and more relevant outputs.
