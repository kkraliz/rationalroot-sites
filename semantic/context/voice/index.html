The Difference

It's not a podcast — it talks back

We're all used to filling these moments with something to listen to: a podcast, an audiobook, the radio. That's one-way. Voice mode is the opposite — it's a conversation. You think out loud, ask a question, change your mind, and it responds to you, in real time.

That small shift changes what the time is good for. A podcast pours information at you on its own schedule. A conversation lets you work something out — chase a half-formed idea, get a thing explained the way you need it, or talk a decision through until it's clear. The only new skill is being willing to speak rather than just listen.

Where It Fits

The gaps where hands are busy but the mind is free

Look for the low-attention stretches of your day — the tasks that run on autopilot and leave plenty of mental room spare. Those are the moments to talk.

✓

The long drive

Open highway and light traffic, where driving is largely automatic and you'd otherwise reach for the radio.

✓

Housework & chores

Dishes, laundry, tidying, cooking — hands fully occupied, brain barely engaged. Talk while you work.

✓

Waiting & queues

The school pickup, the waiting room, the slow checkout. Turn dead time into something useful.

✓

A walk or errand

A loop around the block to clear your head, or the walk to the shops — earbuds in, thinking out loud.

⚠

A note on driving — safety first, always

If the moment you're filling is behind the wheel, treat it exactly like a hands-free phone call. Set it up before you pull away, keep your eyes on the road and hands on the wheel, and stop talking the instant traffic, weather, or your own attention needs it. Driving is the task; the conversation is the passenger. If you couldn't safely take a phone call right then, this waits.

What To Do With It

Good ways to spend the time

Voice mode shows its key points on screen as it speaks, but you never need to look — it's built to be heard. Whatever the task in your hands, these are the conversations worth having while your mind is free.

01

Think a problem through

Talk through a decision, a tricky email, or a plan. Saying it out loud and having something push back often unlocks what staring at a screen couldn't.

02

Capture ideas before they vanish

The shower-thought problem, solved. Dictate the idea, ask it to be tightened up, and arrive with notes already half-written.

03

Learn on the move

Ask for a plain-English explainer on anything — a concept, a company, a place you're curious about — and ask follow-ups like you would a knowledgeable companion.

04

Prep for what's ahead

Rehearse a pitch, warm up for a difficult conversation, or pre-brief a meeting so you walk in already clear on what you want to say.

Our Recommendation

Keep a foot in each camp

We tell clients the same thing: in these early days of AI tools, don't marry a single provider. Hold accounts with more than one — Anthropic, OpenAI, and the others worth watching — and use them deliberately for different jobs.

The pattern we see working is Claude for work, ChatGPT for personal. That isn't a rule about which is "better" — it's about keeping your professional and personal AI lives cleanly separated, and staying fluent in more than one tool while the landscape is still shifting under our feet.

Why Two (Or More)

The tools are moving fast — keep your options open

Capabilities, pricing, and the lead between providers change month to month. Anchoring everything to one account means re-learning everything the day it falls behind. Keeping a live account on each major provider costs little and means you can always reach for the best tool for the task in front of you.

It also keeps a clean line between contexts: work conversations live in one place, personal ones in another. Separate accounts, separate histories, separate billing — and far less worry about what got mixed where.

Getting Started

Set it up before your hands are full

Four steps to get talking

1

Open the app and find voice mode. In Claude, tap the sound-wave icon next to the microphone in the message bar. ChatGPT has an equivalent voice button.

2

Get the audio sorted. Pop in earbuds, or pair to a speaker or car audio via Bluetooth, so you can hear and be heard with the phone out of your hands.

3

Start before you're occupied. Open the conversation, then pick up the laundry or pull away. Don't go hunting for buttons once your hands are busy.

4

Keep it hands-off. Just talk, like someone's in the room with you. Let anything that needs the screen wait until you're free.

The Catch

Voice mode lives in chat — and chat is its own silo

When you open voice mode in the Claude or ChatGPT app, you're talking to chat. What you say is captured there — but it largely stays there.

Every place you use AI keeps its own memory, and they don't share. Claude's chat builds a memory across your conversations, refreshed roughly once a day — but that lives in the chat apps: web, desktop, and mobile, including voice. It doesn't automatically reach Claude Code, which works from its own instruction files and notes, or a Cowork workspace, which keeps its own memory. ChatGPT works the same way inside OpenAI's world.

So speaking gets the thought out of your head — good — but it's parked in one app's chat history. To make it useful everywhere your agents actually do work, it has to be piped into a memory they can all read.
